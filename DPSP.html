Introduction to DPSP

Directive Principles of State Policy (DPSP) are contained in Part IV, Articles 36–51 of the Indian Constitution. They are directives to the state for establishing a just social, economic, and political order.

Origin: DPSPs were borrowed from the Irish Constitution of 1937, which in turn derived the concept from the Spanish Constitution. Dr. B.R. Ambedkar called them "novel features" of the Constitution.

Nature of DPSPs TNPSC ★

  • Non-justiciable: Cannot be enforced by courts (unlike Fundamental Rights)
  • Fundamental in governance: "Fundamental in the governance of the country" — Article 37
  • Duty of State: It shall be the duty of the State to apply these principles in making laws
  • Positive obligations: Unlike FRs (which are mostly negative — "State shall not"), DPSPs are positive obligations on the State
  • Welfare state ideal: Reflect the socio-economic goals of a welfare state
Article 37: "The provisions contained in this Part shall not be enforceable by any court, but the principles therein laid down are nevertheless fundamental in the governance of the country and it shall be the duty of the State to apply these principles in making laws."

Importance of DPSPs UPSC

  • Provide positive obligations to build a welfare state
  • Act as a yardstick to measure the performance of governments
  • Supplement Fundamental Rights by providing socio-economic justice
  • Serve as guidelines for legislation and policy-making
  • Help courts in interpretation of laws — laws implementing DPSPs are favoured
  • Enable India to achieve the goals of Preamble — socialist, secular democratic republic

What is "State" under DPSP? TNPSC ★

Article 36 defines "State" for Part IV purposes. It has the same meaning as in Article 12 (Part III): includes Government and Parliament of India, Government and Legislature of every state, and all local or other authorities within India's territory or under India's Government control.

Granville Austin (constitutional historian) described Fundamental Rights and DPSPs as the "Conscience of the Constitution" — together they form an integrated scheme for establishing a just society. He called them a "seamless web" — separating them would destroy the fabric.

Classification Sources

Dr. Ambedkar broadly classified DPSPs into three groups, which later scholars refined into:

  • Socialistic principles — reflect socialist ideology
  • Gandhian principles — reflect Gandhiji's constructive programme
  • Liberal-Intellectual principles — reflect the liberal political tradition

Social & economic democracy

The Conflict: The central question: Can Parliament pass a law implementing DPSP that infringes upon Fundamental Rights? Or do FRs take absolute precedence? This conflict led to several landmark constitutional amendments and Supreme Court judgments.

Constitutional Resolution — Timeline UPSC

1951 — 1st Amendment
State of Madras v Champakam Dorairajan (1951): SC held FRs prevail over DPSP. Parliament responded with 1st Amendment adding 9th Schedule to protect land reform laws.
1967 — Golaknath Case
Golaknath v State of Punjab: SC held Parliament cannot amend Part III (FRs) at all. FRs are sacrosanct. This created a major impasse.
1971 — 24th Amendment
Parliament added "notwithstanding anything in this Constitution" clause to Article 368 — asserting right to amend any part including FRs. Also amended Art 13 to exclude Art 368.
1972 — 25th Amendment
Added Art 31C: Laws giving effect to Art 39(b) and 39(c) — distributive justice — cannot be challenged on grounds of violating Art 14, 19. Directive principles given priority over these FRs.
1973 — Kesavananda Bharati
Kesavananda Bharati v State of Kerala: 13-judge bench. Parliament can amend Constitution including FRs, but CANNOT destroy the Basic Structure. Upheld 24th Amendment, partially upheld 25th Amendment (removed the non-justiciability clause).
1976 — 42nd Amendment
Extended Art 31C: gave primacy to ALL DPSPs (not just Art 39 b&c) over Art 14, 19, and 31. Tried to make DPSPs superior to all FRs.
1980 — Minerva Mills
Minerva Mills v Union of India: SC struck down the 42nd Amendment extension. Restored balance — harmony between FRs and DPSP is part of the Basic Structure. Neither can be made absolutely superior to the other.
Present — Harmonious Interpretation
Courts now apply harmonious construction — read FRs and DPSPs together; laws reasonably restricting FRs to implement DPSPs are upheld if they don't violate basic structure.
Current Position (Post-Minerva Mills): The Constitution requires a harmonious relationship between FRs and DPSP. Neither is absolutely superior. Laws implementing DPSPs under Art 39(b)/(c) (Art 31C) can override Art 14 and Art 19, but all laws must respect the Basic Structure of the Constitution.

Also changed the word "sovereign democratic republic" to "sovereign socialist secular democratic republic" in the Preamble.

New Directive Principles Added by Amendments

  • Art 39A — Equal justice and free legal aid (42nd Amendment, 1976)
  • Art 43A — Participation of workers in management (42nd Amendment, 1976)
  • Art 43B — Promotion of cooperative societies (97th Amendment, 2011)
  • Art 48A — Environmental protection (42nd Amendment, 1976)
  • Art 38(2) — Minimise inequalities in income, status, opportunities (44th Amendment, 1978)

Landmark Cases on DPSP

1. State of Madras v Champakam Dorairajan (1951) TNPSC ★

Issue: Madras government's communal reservation in medical college seats (based on Art 46 DPSP). Champakam, a Brahmin woman, was denied admission.
Held: Art 29(2) FR (no discrimination in state educational institutions) prevails over Art 46 DPSP. FRs are supreme over DPSPs.
Impact: Led to 1st Constitutional Amendment adding 9th Schedule.

2. State of Bihar v Kameshwar Singh (1952)

Issue: Bihar Zamindari Abolition Act challenged.
Held: Land reform laws implementing DPSPs valid even if they impair property right. Courts began balancing FRs with DPSPs in legislative context.

3. I.C. Golaknath v State of Punjab (1967) UPSC

Issue: Can Parliament amend Fundamental Rights?
Held: NO — Parliament cannot amend Part III (FRs). FRs are eternal and inalienable. A constitutional amendment is a "law" under Art 13, so cannot abridge FRs.
Impact: Led to 24th Amendment overriding this judgment.

4. Kesavananda Bharati v State of Kerala (1973) UPSC

Issue: Validity of 24th, 25th, 29th amendments; Parliament's power to amend FRs.
Held: Parliament CAN amend FRs but CANNOT destroy the Basic Structure of the Constitution. This is the most important constitutional case in Indian history.
DPSP Impact: Art 31C (25th Amendment) upheld for Art 39(b)/(c) but the non-justiciability clause struck down. Harmony between FRs and DPSPs is part of Basic Structure.

5. Minerva Mills Ltd. v Union of India (1980) UPSC

Issue: 42nd Amendment's extension of Art 31C to ALL DPSPs challenged.
Held: The extension was UNCONSTITUTIONAL. Harmony and balance between FRs and DPSPs is part of the Basic Structure. Neither can be made superior to destroy the other. Any law giving absolute primacy to DPSPs over FRs violates basic structure.
Key Quote: "Rights and Directives are like two wheels of a chariot."

6. Waman Rao v Union of India (1981)

Issue: Validity of laws in 9th Schedule after Kesavananda.
Held: Laws included in 9th Schedule before April 24, 1973 (Kesavananda date) are fully protected. Laws added AFTER that date can be examined on Basic Structure violation.

7. Bandhua Mukti Morcha v Union of India (1984)

Issue: Bonded labour system violation.
Held: Art 23 (FR against forced labour) + Art 39(e), 41, 42, 43 (DPSPs) read together to hold that the right to live with dignity includes protection from bonded labour. Showed how FRs and DPSPs can complement each other.

8. Air India v Nergesh Meerza (1981) & Equal Pay Cases

Art 39(d) — Equal Pay for Equal Work: Though not a fundamental right by itself, courts have used Art 39(d) + Art 14 (equality) together to enforce equal pay as a constitutional right in service matters.

9. M.C. Mehta Cases (Environmental Cases)

Art 48A (environment protection DPSP) has been used extensively in PIL cases. SC expanded Art 21 (right to life FR) to include right to clean environment, reading it with Art 48A and Art 51A(g) (fundamental duty).

10. Mohd. Ahmed Khan v Shah Bano Begum (1985)

Art 44 — Uniform Civil Code: SC recommended UCC implementation but Parliament passed the Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Divorce) Act 1986, limiting the judgment. Controversy over Art 44 continues as an unimplemented DPSP.

Implementation of DPSPs

Though not enforceable by courts, DPSPs have been substantially implemented through legislation and government schemes over 75 years of independence.

Unimplemented / Partially Implemented DPSPs UPSC

  • Art 44 (UCC): Most debated unimplemented DPSP — personal laws still vary by religion
  • Art 43 (Living Wage): Minimum wage system exists but falls short of a true living wage for all workers
  • Art 47 (Prohibition): Only a few states have prohibition (Gujarat); most states have not implemented
Tamil Nadu Angle (TNPSC): Tamil Nadu has implemented several DPSPs significantly — Panchayati Raj (Art 40), free legal aid through TNSLA, TASMAC revenue vs prohibition debate (Art 47), comprehensive welfare schemes for SC/ST (Art 46), Mid-day Meal Scheme (Art 45/47).
